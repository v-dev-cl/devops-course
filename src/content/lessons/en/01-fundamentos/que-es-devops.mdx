DevOps is not a job title, a team, or Kubernetes. It is a way of working where
the same people (or teams that genuinely collaborate) build, deploy, and
operate the software. The question it answers is: **why does it take us weeks
to ship a change to production that took an hour to write?**

## The problem: silos

In the classic model, development writes code and "throws it over the wall" to
operations, who then have to deploy it and keep it alive without having written
it. The incentives end up crossed: dev wants to change fast, ops wants nothing
to change because changes break production. The result is giant, infrequent,
terrifying releases.

If you work alone or operate your own services, you still have this problem in
miniature: every manual step between your commit and production —compiling by
hand, copying a `.env`, running `kubectl apply` from your machine— is a little
wall. DevOps is about tearing them down with automation and feedback.

## CALMS: a yardstick to measure against

The CALMS model summarizes the five fronts of DevOps:

- **C**ulture: the people who write the code are responsible for it running in
  production ("you build it, you run it").
- **A**utomation: everything repeatable gets automated: build, test, deploy,
  infrastructure provisioning.
- **L**ean: small batches. It is better to deploy 10 small changes than 1 giant
  one.
- **M**easurement: if you do not measure deploys, errors, and recovery times,
  you do not know whether you are improving.
- **S**haring: operational knowledge does not live in one person's head; it
  lives in runbooks, dashboards, and postmortems.

## Apply it to your case

Look at your current flow honestly. For example, a common setup: GitHub Actions
builds the image and pushes it to GHCR (automation ✔), but the deploy is a
manual `skaffold deploy` from your laptop (automation ✘), there are no
deploy-frequency metrics (measurement ✘), and the knowledge of "how this gets
deployed" lives only in your memory (sharing ✘). That gap between automated CI
and manual deploy is exactly what you will close in modules 4 and 5.
