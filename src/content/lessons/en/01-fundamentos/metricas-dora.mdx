How do you know your DevOps is improving and not just changing? The **DORA**
research program (DevOps Research and Assessment) found four metrics that
predict the performance of a delivery team. They are **outcome metrics**, not
vanity metrics: they do not measure how many lines you write or how many
meetings you have, but how fast and how safely you deliver value.

## The four metrics

- **Deployment frequency**: how often you put code into production. Elite
  deploys on demand, multiple times a day; low, less than once a month.
- **Lead time for changes**: how long it takes from making a `commit` to that
  change running in production. Elite: under an hour; low: more than a month.
- **Change failure rate**: what percentage of your deploys causes an incident
  that requires remediation (a `rollback`, a hotfix). Elite/high: 0–15%; low:
  much higher.
- **MTTR** (mean time to restore): how long you take to recover the service when
  a change breaks production. Elite: under an hour; low: more than a week.

The first two measure **speed**; the last two, **stability**. DORA's key finding
is that they are not opposed: elite teams are fast *and* stable at the same
time. Going slow does not make you safer.

## Why they are outcome, not vanity, metrics

A vanity metric ("we wrote 5,000 lines this week") can go up while the product
gets worse. DORA metrics measure the outcome that really matters —delivering
working changes, fast and without breaking things— so you cannot fake them by
working more hours. If your lead time drops from a week to a day, that is real
for whoever is waiting on the feature.

## Estimate them for your flow today

Apply them to your current setup of dozens of microservices with manual deploys:

- **Deployment frequency**: count how many times you ran `skaffold deploy` last
  week. If it was "whenever I remembered", you are probably medium or low.
- **Lead time**: measure the time between the `commit` you merged and the moment
  you ran `skaffold deploy` with that image. If you deploy by hand, that gap is
  usually hours or days, not minutes.
- **Change failure rate**: of your last 10 deploys, how many forced you into a
  hotfix or rollback? That percentage is your rate.
- **MTTR**: the last time a deploy broke a service, how long did it take you to
  stabilize it?

One important detail: **actually measuring deployment frequency requires
automated deploys**. If you rely on your memory to note every `skaffold deploy`,
the data will be poor. That is why measurement and automation go together: when
the deploy runs through a pipeline, the metric records itself.
