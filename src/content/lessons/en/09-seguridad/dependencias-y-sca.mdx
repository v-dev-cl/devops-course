The previous lesson left you with one idea: most of your attack surface lives in
code you **didn't write**. Your NestJS `worker` imports a handful of direct
packages, but each one drags in its own, and those drag in theirs. A typical
`node_modules` ends up with hundreds —sometimes thousands— of packages, and any
of them can carry a CVE. **SCA** (Software Composition Analysis) is the scanner
that audits that tree.

## SCA is not SAST

They are different scanners looking at different things:

- **SAST** reads **your own code** and hunts for dangerous patterns you wrote
  (the Semgrep lesson).
- **SCA** reads **your dependencies** and compares them against databases of
  known CVEs. It doesn't care how you wrote your code; it cares which version of
  which package you have installed.

A green SAST scan says nothing about your dependencies, and vice versa. You need
both: they cover surfaces that don't overlap.

## The transitive risk

Here is the uncomfortable part. When you add a package to your `package.json`,
**you chose that one**. But the CVE that will hit you is almost never in what you
chose: it's three levels down, in a **transitive** dependency you never named.
You imported a web framework, it pulls in a parser, that parser pulls in a
utility library, and there —in the utility you didn't even know existed— is the
hole. Log4Shell was exactly this at massive scale: thousands of vulnerable
applications because of a logging library almost nobody had added by hand.

The practical takeaway: auditing your direct dependencies isn't enough. SCA
scans the **whole tree**, direct and transitive, because that's where the real
risk lives.

## Scanning with Trivy (and Grype)

**Trivy** (from Aqua Security) scans an entire filesystem, and while doing so it
reads the **lockfiles** (`pnpm-lock.yaml`, `package-lock.json`, `go.sum`, etc.)
to know exactly which versions are resolved. The base command is:

```bash
trivy fs .
```

`fs` is for *filesystem*: it points at the current directory, finds the
lockfiles, resolves the full tree, and reports every package with a known CVE,
with its severity. **Grype** (from Anchore) is an alternative SCA scanner that
does the same over the lockfile; if you want a second pair of eyes, you run both
and compare. The logic is identical: lockfile → resolved tree → match against
CVEs.

## Triage: severity and "fixable vs not"

The first scan of an old repo always throws a long list. You don't fix it all at
once; you **triage** it:

- **Severity.** Trivy classifies each finding (`LOW`, `MEDIUM`, `HIGH`,
  `CRITICAL`). You start with `CRITICAL` and `HIGH`: that's where the risk that
  matters lives.
- **Fixable vs not.** If a patched version exists, the fix is a version bump
  —cheap, do it now. If there is **no** patch yet (the "fixed version" column is
  empty), you can't bump: you note the risk, assess whether the code actually
  reaches that vulnerable path, and monitor until the patch ships.

Triaging by severity and by fixability is what keeps an honest scan from
becoming a wall of noise nobody reads.

## Keeping it reproducible: lockfile and Dependabot

A scan is only useful if it's **reproducible**: today's result on your machine
has to match CI's result tomorrow. The **lockfile** is what gives you that. Your
`package.json` may say `^4.18.0` —a range— but `pnpm-lock.yaml` **pins** the
exact version that was installed, including every transitive. Trivy reads that
pin, so the scan is deterministic: same input, same result. Without a lockfile,
two installs could resolve different versions and the scan becomes a lottery.

But pinning freezes the tree: if you never update, new CVEs pile up silently.
That's where **Dependabot** comes in. It watches your lockfiles, and when a new
CVE appears or a release fixes one, it **opens a PR** with the bump (and raises a
security alert on the repo). You review the PR, let CI run the tests, and merge.
The lockfile gives you reproducible scans; Dependabot keeps you current without
auditing by hand.

## The CI job

The local run becomes a gate in the module-4 pipeline. The stable form today is
the official Trivy action:

```yaml
  sca:
    runs-on: ubuntu-latest
    steps:
      - uses: actions/checkout@v4
      - name: Trivy SCA (gate)
        uses: aquasecurity/trivy-action@v0.24.0
        with:
          scan-type: fs
          scan-ref: .
          # Fail the build only on HIGH/CRITICAL: that's the gate
          exit-code: '1'
          severity: HIGH,CRITICAL
```

The option that turns the scan into a **gate** is `--exit-code 1 --severity
HIGH,CRITICAL` (here expressed as `exit-code` + `severity` in the action): the
job fails —and blocks the merge— only when there's a `HIGH` or `CRITICAL` CVE.
`LOW`/`MEDIUM` still show up in the report so you can see them, but they don't
fail the build. It's the same anti alert-fatigue discipline: the gate fires on
what truly matters, not on every minor finding.
