Your data is the one truly irreplaceable thing in your stack. AWS has two services
that are likely your first adoption candidates: **S3** for files and **RDS** for
relational databases.

## S3: object storage

S3 stores **objects** (a file + metadata) in **buckets**. It's not a disk (that's
**block storage**, EBS) nor a shared filesystem (that's **file storage**, EFS):
it's an HTTP API to upload and download blobs. Ideal for **product images** and
**backups**: things you write once and read many times.

Its headline number is **11 nines of durability (99.999999999%)**: AWS replicates
each object across multiple AZs. Losing an object is statistically almost
impossible. Note: durability (not losing the data) is not the same as availability
(always being able to access it).

### Storage classes

You pick a class by access frequency, balancing storage cost vs access cost:

- **S3 Standard** — frequent access (product images served daily).
- **S3 Standard-IA** (Infrequent Access) — cheaper to store, pricier to read; for
  data you touch rarely.
- **S3 Glacier / Deep Archive** — cold archiving, dirt cheap to store but with
  minutes-to-hours wait to retrieve; ideal for old backups you almost never read
  but must retain.

### Presigned URLs and lifecycle rules

To let a user upload or download an object **without** giving them AWS
credentials, you generate a **presigned URL**: a temporary, signed link that grants
scoped permission to that object for a while. Your backend signs it; the client
uploads straight to S3 without the file passing through your server.

**Lifecycle rules** automate the object's life: "objects under this prefix, move
them to Glacier after 30 days and delete them after 365." For your backups, that's
automatic retention with no cron to maintain.

## RDS vs your self-hosted Mongo/Postgres

RDS is a **managed relational database** (Postgres, MySQL, etc.): AWS handles
patches, automated backups, Multi-AZ failover, and read replicas. You're left to
operate your schema and your queries.

Your reality is **self-managed Mongo on your VPS**. Weigh this honestly:

- RDS is **relational**; Mongo is document-based. The managed equivalent of Mongo
  on AWS is **DocumentDB** (compatible with the Mongo API, not real Mongo).
- **What you'd buy**: backups and failover without operating them, Multi-AZ (a
  synchronous replica in another AZ with automatic failover), and read replicas to
  scale heavy reads.
- **What you would NOT buy**: it costs **more**, there's less fine-grained control
  (you don't get into the host), and DocumentDB doesn't cover 100% of Mongo's
  features. If your Mongo runs fine and you have decent backup discipline,
  RDS/DocumentDB solves a pain you may not have.

## Backup/restore discipline

A backup you've never restored isn't a backup, it's a hope. RDS automates
snapshots, but you still must **test the restore** periodically. If you stay on
self-hosted Mongo, that discipline (dump, off-site copy, test restore) is on you
—it's exactly the burden a managed database takes off your plate.

## The egress trap, again

If a **chatty** app (many small queries or large, frequent reads) ends up **crossing
the internet** between your k3s on your VPS and an RDS/S3 in AWS, egress can get very
expensive. The rule: **data and compute live close together**. If you move the
database to AWS, consider moving the service that hits it most too, or the
operational savings get eaten by transfer.
