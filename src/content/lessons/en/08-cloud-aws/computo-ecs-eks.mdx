You already run Kubernetes well on-premise: k3s, ArgoCD, ESO, kustomize. That
completely changes the compute decision on AWS, because your investment in k8s is
an **asset**, not something you want to throw away.

## The compute spectrum

From rawest to most managed:

- **EC2** — virtual machines. You install, patch, and operate everything on top.
  Maximum control, maximum operational burden. It's the "self-managed VM" of AWS.
- **ECS + Fargate** — a container orchestrator **native to AWS**. With Fargate you
  don't manage nodes: you declare a *task* (container + CPU/memory) and AWS runs
  the container without you seeing the VM. Simple, but **new vocabulary** (task
  definition, service, ECS cluster) and some **lock-in** to AWS.
- **EKS** — **managed Kubernetes**. AWS operates the control plane (API server,
  etcd); you bring your nodes (or Fargate) and your manifests. Your ArgoCD, ESO,
  and kustomize **work the same** as on k3s.

## The honest table for someone who already knows k8s

| Option | Keeps your k8s investment | Operational burden | Base cost | Lock-in |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| EC2 (k3s yourself) | Yes | High (you operate all) | Just the VMs | Low |
| ECS + Fargate | No (new vocabulary) | Low | Per task | High |
| EKS | **Yes** (ArgoCD/ESO/kustomize) | Medium | **~$73/mo** + nodes | Medium |

The **EKS control plane costs ~$0.10/hour ≈ ~$73/month**, *plus* the cost of the
nodes (EC2 or Fargate) separately. Compared to your k3s on your VPS —where the
control plane costs you nothing extra— it's a new fixed charge to justify.

## When to migrate to EKS and when not to

**EKS makes sense if**: you need to be in AWS for proximity to other AWS services
(an RDS, SQS queues), you want to stop operating the control plane, and you value
keeping your GitOps stack as-is. The curve is gentle because you already know k8s.

**Stay on k3s if**: your cluster runs fine, the cost works for you, and you have no
strong reason to be inside AWS. **Don't migrate for fashion.** 35 happy services on
k3s on your own VPS is a setup hard to beat on cost. EKS doesn't make you "more DevOps";
it just changes who operates the control plane in exchange for money.

## Lambda for event glue

Lambda runs **functions** serverless, triggered by events (an object landing in
S3, a message on a queue, a cron). It's not for your long-lived NestJS services
—it's for **glue**: short, sporadic tasks. A function like `fetch-worker` is exactly that
case: a function that fires, does its job, and dies, with no pod running 24/7. You
pay per invocation and per ms of execution; if it doesn't run, you don't pay.

## Spot instances

**Spot instances** are spare EC2 capacity at 60–90% off, with the caveat that **AWS
can reclaim them with ~2 minutes' notice**. Perfect for interruption-tolerant
workloads: batch jobs, queue workers that can retry, CI environments. **Not** for
anything that can't die abruptly. On EKS it's common to have a spot node group for
the workers and an on-demand one for the critical stuff.
