Your current stack —dozens of NestJS services on k3s on your own VPS— is **cheap and yours**.
Start from that premise: AWS is not here to replace it, but for you to adopt
**selectively** the services where the operational burden hurts. A lift-and-shift
(moving everything as-is) usually ends up more expensive and more complex than
your setup. The right question isn't "should I go to the cloud?", but "which
piece is worth having someone else operate for me?".

## The map: account, region, AZ

Three levels organize everything in AWS:

- **Account** — your billing and isolation boundary. Every resource lives in an
  account and is billed there. Serious teams use several accounts (dev, prod).
- **Region** — a set of datacenters in a geographic area, with its code:
  `us-east-1` (Virginia), `sa-east-1` (São Paulo). You pick a region by latency,
  by cost (prices vary across regions), and by data residency.
- **Availability Zone (AZ)** — within a region there are several AZs, which are
  physically separate datacenters connected with low latency. Spreading across
  several AZs is what gives you high availability if one goes down.

## Shared responsibility

AWS operates under the **shared responsibility model**: AWS is responsible for
security **of** the cloud (hardware, datacenters, hypervisor) and you for security
**in** the cloud (your data, your IAM permissions, patching your EC2 instances). A
misconfigured, public S3 bucket is your fault, not AWS's.

## The Chile context: real latency

There's no full AWS region in Chile. The closest is **`sa-east-1` (São Paulo)**,
with an RTT on the order of ~40–60 ms from Santiago. For comparison, `us-east-1`
(Virginia) is around ~150 ms. There's also the **Santiago Local Zone (`scl1`,
whose parent is `us-east-1`)**: a compute and network extension to serve low local
latency, but with a reduced service catalog —don't expect everything a full region
has.

This matters for you: if a piece is **chatty** (many round trips) with your k3s on
your European VPS (Germany), putting it in `sa-east-1` won't fix the latency toward your
cluster. Whatever you migrate should be self-contained or latency-tolerant.

## The ~6 services to start with

You don't need the catalog of 200+ services. These six orient you:

- **IAM** — identity and permissions. The security foundation of everything (next lesson).
- **VPC** — your private network in AWS: subnets, routing, firewalls.
- **EC2** — virtual machines, raw compute.
- **S3** — object storage (product images, backups).
- **RDS** — managed databases (Postgres, MySQL).
- **Secrets Manager** — managed secrets (the module's deep-dive, with ESO).

## CapEx/ops vs OpEx/managed, honestly

Your VPS is **CapEx-ish**: you pay a low fixed cost, but **you operate**
(patches, backups, HA, monitoring). AWS is **OpEx**: you pay per use, pricier per
unit, but the provider operates the managed piece. The trade isn't "cheap vs
expensive": it's **money vs operations time**. A Multi-AZ RDS costs several times
what a Postgres on a VM does, but it takes failover and backups off your plate.
That math only adds up where operating genuinely hurts.

## Free tier and the egress trap

The **free tier** comes in three flavors: always-free (with limits), 12 months
free (new account), and one-off trials. It's for learning, not for production.

The classic cost trap is **egress**: AWS charges you for traffic that **leaves**
toward the internet (inbound is usually free). A chatty pattern between your k3s
and an S3 or RDS in AWS can produce a surprise transfer bill. Before moving
anything, estimate: how many GB leave per month and at what price per GB in that
region. Compute is easy to predict; egress is what inflates the bills you didn't
expect.
