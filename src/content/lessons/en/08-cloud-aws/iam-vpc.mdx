IAM (identity and permissions) and VPC (the network) are the two foundations
everything else in AWS rests on. Understand them and the rest of the services slot
on top without surprises.

## IAM: users vs roles vs policies

Three concepts, three responsibilities:

- **User** — a long-lived human identity, with fixed credentials (console password
  and/or access keys). Use it for people, ideally with MFA.
- **Role** — an identity **without permanent credentials** that something *assumes*
  temporarily and receives expiring credentials for. It's the right thing for
  **workloads**: an app on EC2/ECS/EKS assumes a role and never holds a fixed key.
- **Policy** — a JSON document that says **which actions** are allowed on **which
  resources**. It attaches to users, groups, or roles.

The golden rule: **roles for workloads, never long-lived access keys** inside an
app. A leaked static key (in git, in a log, in a Docker image) is the number-one
breach in AWS. Roles hand out temporary credentials that rotate on their own.

## Anatomy of a policy: least privilege

Least privilege = you grant exactly the needed permissions, not one more. This
policy grants **read-only** on **one** specific S3 bucket, nothing else:

```json
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [
    {
      "Sid": "ReadOnlyOneBucket",
      "Effect": "Allow",
      "Action": ["s3:GetObject", "s3:ListBucket"],
      "Resource": [
        "arn:aws:s3:::shop-product-images",
        "arn:aws:s3:::shop-product-images/*"
      ]
    }
  ]
}
```

Note the two `Resource` entries: the bucket **itself** (`arn:...:bucket`) for
`ListBucket`, and the **objects inside** (`/*`) for `GetObject`. A common mistake
is listing only one and having it fail. And never use `"Resource": "*"` with
`"Action": "s3:*"` unless you really mean to grant everything on everything.

## VPC: your private network

A **VPC** is an isolated virtual network with its own IP range (e.g. `10.0.0.0/16`).
Inside it you split into **subnets**:

- **Public subnet** — has a route to an *Internet Gateway*; whatever lives there
  can receive internet traffic (a load balancer, a bastion host).
- **Private subnet** — has no direct route to the internet; that's where your apps
  and databases go. If they need to *reach out* to the internet (e.g. call an API),
  they do so through a **NAT Gateway** placed in the public subnet. The **route
  tables** are what define these paths.

The standard pattern: load balancer in the public subnet, app and RDS in the
private subnet. The database never exposed to the internet.

## Security groups vs NACLs (your on-prem intuition)

Here your experience operating servers maps directly:

- **Security group** ≈ **stateful firewall per resource**. You attach it to an
  instance/ENI; if you allow inbound traffic, the outbound response is allowed
  automatically (it's *stateful*, it remembers the connection). It only has *allow*
  rules. It's your day-to-day tool.
- **NACL** (Network ACL) — a **stateless firewall at the subnet level**, with allow
  *and* deny rules, evaluated by number. Stateless means you must allow inbound
  **and** outbound explicitly. It's rarely used; security groups cover almost
  everything.

If you come from `ufw`/iptables on your VPS, a security group is basically
that, but managed and attached to the resource.
