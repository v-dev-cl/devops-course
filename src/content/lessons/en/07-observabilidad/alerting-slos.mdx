An alert has exactly one job: make a person drop what they're doing. If it fires
and there's nothing to do, or nobody is affected, that alert is training you to
ignore it. Most alerting problems aren't technical: they're judgment calls about
*what* deserves to wake you up.

## Symptoms vs. causes

A **cause-based** alert fires on something internal: "CPU over 90%", "a pod
restarted". The trouble is that many causes don't matter to the user —a pod can
restart and the service keep answering perfectly— and many symptoms have no
cause you anticipated.

A **symptom-based** alert fires on what the user experiences: "5% of requests to
`/search` are failing", "p99 latency crossed 2 s". **Alert on symptoms.** The
mental rule: *page only on user pain* —let the phone ring only when someone out
there is feeling it. CPU usage can be a dashboard you look at while investigating,
not an alert that wakes you up.

## Alert fatigue

When everything alerts, nothing alerts. A channel that gets 200 notifications a
day becomes background noise and the real alert gets lost among the false ones.
The cure is brutal but simple: every alert that didn't lead to an action is a
candidate to delete or downgrade. Separate **severities**: `critical` pages by
phone (active user-facing loss); `warning` goes to a channel you check during
business hours.

## Alertmanager: the basics

Prometheus evaluates the alert rules and, when one fires, sends the alert to
**Alertmanager**, which decides what to do with it. Three concepts:

- **Routes** — a tree that routes each alert to a receiver based on its labels.
  `severity="critical"` goes to PagerDuty; `warning` goes to Slack.
- **Grouping** — bundles related alerts into a single notification. If 30 pods
  of the same service fail, you get *one* message, not 30.
- **Silences** — you temporarily mute alerts matching certain labels. Planned
  maintenance on `pricing`: you silence its alerts for two hours and wake nobody
  for something you already know about.

## SLI → SLO → error budget

Here's the chain that gives alerting its rigor:

- **SLI** (indicator): a measure of service health as the user sees it. E.g.:
  *fraction of requests to `/search` served in under 500 ms*.
- **SLO** (objective): the target on that SLI over a window. E.g.: *99% of
  requests to `/search` under 500 ms over 30 days*.
- **Error budget**: the complement of the SLO, what you *can afford* to get
  wrong. If the target is 99%, the budget is **1%**.

### The concrete math

Say `/search` gets **2,000,000 requests** over 30 days. SLO = 99% under 500 ms.
The error budget is 1%:

```text
budget = (1 − 0.99) × 2,000,000 = 20,000 "bad" requests allowed over 30 days
```

You have room for 20,000 slow or failed requests in the month. If by the first
week you've already burned 18,000, there's almost nothing left: that's the signal
to **freeze risky releases** and stabilize. If you're at 2,000 halfway through
the month, you have budget to spare to deploy aggressively. The budget turns
"do we deploy on Friday?" into a decision with numbers instead of gut feel.

## Burn rate: the intuition

You don't want to alert only once the budget is gone —by then it's too late— nor
on every single slow request. **Burn rate** measures *how fast* you're consuming
the budget. Burn rate 1× means that, at this pace, you spend exactly the whole
budget over the full window (30 days). Burn rate 10× means you'd spend it 10
times faster: in 3 days you're out. That's why you alert on a high *and
sustained* burn rate: a 10× spike for 30 seconds is noise; 10× sustained for an
hour is a real incident worth a page. This gives you alerts that fire early when
the damage is fast and stay quiet on passing noise.
