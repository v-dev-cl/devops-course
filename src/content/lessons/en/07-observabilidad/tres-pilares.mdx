You've got dozens of NestJS services running on k3s. Something is slow. Where do you
start? Observability rests on three kinds of signal, and each answers a
different question. Confusing them is expensive: you end up paying to store
gigabytes of logs when what you needed was a one-line metric.

## The three questions

- **Logs — what happened?** Discrete events with detail: "the `sync-offers` job
  failed with `ECONNREFUSED` at 14:32." They're your narrative: high resolution,
  high cost per event.
- **Metrics — how much, and when?** Numbers aggregated over time: requests per
  second, p95 latency, memory used. Cheap to store (a time series is just
  numbers), perfect for dashboards and alerts.
- **Traces — where, in which link of the chain?** The journey of a single
  request across several services: the gateway called `catalog`, which called
  `pricing`, which waited 800 ms on Mongo. They answer "where did the time go?"
  when a request crosses several services.

## When each one is the wrong tool

Metrics **don't** tell you *why*. You see the error rate climbed to 5%, but the
metric holds no stack trace; for that you go to the logs. Logs are **no good**
for answering "what's my p99 latency this week?": you'd have to parse millions
of lines, which is exactly what a histogram-type metric solves cheaply.

The silent enemy is **cardinality**. A metric with a label per `user_id` or
`request_id` spawns millions of time series and blows up Prometheus. Those
high-cardinality identifiers belong in logs and traces, not in metrics. We'll
cover that in detail next lesson.

## Monitoring vs. observability

**Monitoring** is watching questions you already know how to ask: "is the
service up?", "did it cross 80% CPU?". **Observability** is being able to answer
questions you didn't anticipate —"why do *only* southern-region users, *only* in
checkout, see timeouts?"— without shipping new code. Monitoring prepares
dashboards; observability lets you improvise queries when the incident doesn't
look like any previous one.

## How they connect: the correlation id

The three signals become far more useful when they share a thread. If every
incoming request gets a `trace_id` and that id rides along in the logs of every
service it touches and in the trace's spans, then you jump from "I saw an error
on the dashboard" → "here's the full trace" → "here's the log with the exact
stack trace" in seconds. Without that thread you have three piles of data that
don't talk to each other. We go deep on correlation ids in the logs lesson.

## The honest truth about tracing for a solo operator

Logs and metrics give you 90% of the value for little effort: prom-client
exposes metrics in minutes, and your services already write logs. Distributed
**tracing** (OpenTelemetry) is real and powerful, but adopting it costs:
instrumenting every service, running a collector, paying to store spans. For one
person operating dozens of services, don't start there. Adopt tracing **the day
cross-service latency questions start to genuinely hurt** —when "which service
in the chain is slow?" costs you an afternoon of guessing. That day OpenTelemetry
is worth the cost; before it, it's over-engineering.
