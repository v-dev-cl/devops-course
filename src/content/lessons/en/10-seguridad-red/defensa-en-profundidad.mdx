You closed the perimeter. The firewall only lets in an allowlist of IPs: your own
nodes. From the outside, nobody talks to your database or your queues. You feel
covered. But there's a question the allowlist doesn't answer: **what happens when one
of those inside nodes is the one that gets compromised?** In the fictional **DealHive**
fleet, the answer was uncomfortable: `extractors-1` fell, and from there you could
reach everything.

## An allowlist is a north-south control, not east-west

An IP allowlist is a **perimeter** control: it governs the traffic that crosses the
edge, what comes in and goes out (north-south). That's good and you need it. The
problem is what it does **not** govern: the traffic **inside** the perimeter, between
your own machines (east-west).

If your only defense is "only our servers can reach the datastore", then all your
servers are equally trusted by one another. That's a **flat trust zone**: once
you're inside, everything talks to everything without asking anything. The allowlist
drew a high wall around the neighborhood, but inside, no house has a lock.

In DealHive the wall existed: the provider firewall only let the fleet's nodes
through. But `data-1` (the database and cache) and `bus-1` (the queue) asked nobody
for credentials. Why would they, if "only our servers" can reach them?

## A compromised host turns the perimeter into a single line of defense

Assume breach. It isn't pessimism: it's the baseline assumption of any serious
design. A scraper like `extractors-1` runs code that touches hostile HTML all day, it
has dependencies, it has surface. One day one of those services falls —an RCE, a
leaked credential, a poisoned image.

The attacker is now **inside the allowlist**. They have an IP from the allowlist. And
they find a Redis with no password on `data-1` and an open Mongo on `bus-1`, because
they "were on the private network". A single command is enough:

If that returns `PONG`, there's nothing to authenticate: the datastore trusts anyone
who can connect. A Redis, Memcached, Mongo, or Elasticsearch with no auth on the
internal network is the classic recipe for **lateral movement**: the compromised host
hops from one service to the next without ever hitting a single password again. The
perimeter was your only layer, and it's already on the inside.

## Defense in depth: layers that don't depend on the perimeter

Defense in depth means not betting everything on a single control. If the perimeter
falls, let the attacker hit another layer. And another. Three that apply here:

**1. Authenticate and authorize service to service, even inside.** This is the heart
of zero-trust: the network is not an identity. Have `data-1` and `bus-1` require
credentials even when the connection comes from the private network. "Only our
servers can reach it" is not authentication; it's an assumption a compromised host
breaks in a second.

**2. Least privilege.** Each service with its own credential, not one key shared by
the whole fleet. And that credential **scoped**: the scraper's database user writes to
its own table and nothing else; it isn't a cluster admin. If `extractors-1` falls, the
damage reaches as far as *its* permission reaches, not as far as the network reaches.

**3. Segmentation.** Don't let everything talk to everything. Inside the cluster, a
**NetworkPolicy** that allows traffic only between the pods that genuinely need to
talk. Between nodes, separate subnets instead of one flat network. Segmentation cuts
the **blast radius**: even if a host falls, it can't reach half the fleet.

No layer is perfect alone. The allowlist filters the outsiders, auth stops the
insider that shouldn't be there, least privilege limits what they achieve if they get
in, and segmentation contains how far they spread. That redundancy —not one high
wall— is what holds the system up when something fails.

DealHive learned the lesson with `extractors-1`: the perimeter was well closed, but
inside it was a flat zone. Putting a password on `data-1` and `bus-1`, giving each
service its own scoped credential, and segmenting with NetworkPolicy didn't replace
the allowlist: it **complemented** it. The design question is no longer "can they get
in?", but "when one of ours falls, how far does it reach?".
