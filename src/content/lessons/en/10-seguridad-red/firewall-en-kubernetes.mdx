You have a bare-metal Kubernetes —in the fictional **DealHive** fleet, each node
is its own single-node k3s cluster— and you believe the host firewall protects
you. You run `ufw deny` on a port, you see the rule active, and you sleep
soundly. The problem: on k8s that almost never does what you think. This lesson
takes that trap apart and shows you what the real perimeter control is.

## The trap: ufw doesn't see your services' traffic

DealHive exposed `data-1`'s database with a **NodePort** Service. Someone, just
in case, added a ufw rule to deny that port from outside. The rule was there,
visible in `ufw status`. And still, the database kept answering anyone who hit it
from the internet. It wasn't a syntax error or an un-rebooted node: it's **how
Kubernetes networking works**.

When you install a CNI (Calico, Flannel, Cilium, whichever) and kube-proxy, those
components **program their own iptables/nftables rules** on the node. They aren't
cosmetic: they are how a packet arriving at the `NodePort` ends up redirected to
the right pod, wherever it lives.

Here is the detail that explains everything. The NodePort redirection is done
with **DNAT in the PREROUTING chain of the `nat` table**. That chain is
**evaluated before** the `INPUT` chain of the `filter` table, which is where ufw
puts its rules. By the time ufw "should" see the packet, it has already been
rewritten (DNAT) and forwarded toward the pod. The ufw `deny` is never applied to
the service's traffic: it runs too late, in the wrong chain.

The uncomfortable conclusion: **the CNI/kube-proxy rules run first**, so a ufw
rule over a NodePort (or a LoadBalancer, via MetalLB on bare-metal) does not block
it. Your "firewall" is looking the other way.

```
   packet →  PREROUTING (nat)   ← here kube-proxy DNATs to the pod
                   │
                   ▼
              FORWARD ─────────→  pod
                   ╳
              INPUT (filter)     ← here lives the ufw rule: it never sees it
```

## The real perimeter control: the provider firewall

If the host firewall isn't trustworthy for this, what is? The **provider or
external firewall**: the one that lives **outside the host**, ahead of the
machine. In a cloud it's the equivalent of a security group; on bare-metal it can
be the datacenter firewall or a perimeter appliance. The key is that it filters
the packet **before** it touches the CNI chains. Since it doesn't compete with the
node's iptables, its `deny` does win.

A practical rule for DealHive: `data-1`'s NodePort port isn't closed with ufw,
it's closed at the external firewall, allowing through only the origins that
genuinely need to reach it (for example, a documentation IP like `203.0.113.10`).
The perimeter lives outside the node, not inside it.

To audit what you're exposing, start by listing the services across the whole
fleet and looking at which are NodePort or LoadBalancer:

A `ClusterIP` is internal and doesn't worry you from outside. The `NodePort` and
`LoadBalancer` ones are the ones that open a reachable port on the node: those are
your exposure surface, and the ones the external firewall has to cover.

## Inside the cluster: NetworkPolicy

The provider firewall governs the **edge** (who enters the node from outside).
But inside, between pods, the control is another: **NetworkPolicy**. It's a
Kubernetes object that says which pods can talk to which pods, and the **CNI
enforces it** (the same components that complicated things with ufw now play in
your favor). Without NetworkPolicy, all pods talk to each other freely; with it,
you segment east-west traffic and limit what reaches, say, `data-1`'s database
from the rest of the fleet.

Two controls, two planes: the external firewall for the perimeter (north-south),
and NetworkPolicy for pod-to-pod traffic (east-west). The host firewall —ufw,
iptables by hand— **is neither of the two** for k8s service traffic. Internalizing
that is the difference between believing you're protected and being protected.
