You put your site behind a CDN with a WAF. You see rules blocking injections, rate
limiting, bot management. You feel covered. But there's a question almost nobody
asks: **what happens if someone talks directly to your origin server, without going
through the CDN?** If the answer is "it answers anyway", then all that WAF is
decorative. In the fictional **DealHive** fleet, the origin behind `edge-eu` was
still answering on its raw IP, and everything slipped through there.

## A CDN only protects the traffic that passes through it

The idea of a CDN with a WAF is simple: the browser resolves your domain to the
CDN's IPs, the CDN inspects the request, filters it, and only then forwards it to
your **origin** (the real server where your app runs). The WAF lives in the CDN. By
definition, **it can only inspect what passes through the CDN**.

The problem is that your origin is still a machine with its own IP, listening on
80/443. The domain points to the CDN, yes, but the origin's IP doesn't disappear:
it's still there, reachable, if someone knows it. And finding it out is easier than
it looks.

## How the CDN gets bypassed: finding the origin IP

The internet is being scanned all the time. There are services that sweep the entire
IPv4 address space, touch every port, record what answers, and publish a queryable
index —think of search engines like Shodan or Censys, but the mechanism is generic:
anyone can scan. Your origin, if it answers on 443, **gets indexed with its TLS
banner, its certificate, and its behavior**.

Add to that the historical leaks: **passive DNS** records (snapshots of how your
domain used to resolve before you put the CDN in front), the **Certificate
Transparency** logs (every issued certificate is public and lists the names it
covers), an old subdomain pointing at the origin, an email whose headers reveal the
IP. The attacker pieces these clues together, lands on the origin's IP, and talks to
it **directly**:

```
legitimate browser → CDN (WAF inspects) → origin
attacker           → ──────────────────  → origin   (bypassed the WAF)
```

If the origin answers that direct connection, the WAF never saw the request. The
filtering, the rate limiting, the bot management: all bypassed. There was no bug in
the WAF; the traffic simply didn't pass through it.

## The fix: lock the origin to the CDN ranges

The real defense isn't in the CDN, it's at the **origin**: have it reject any
connection that doesn't come from the CDN. Serious CDNs **publish their egress IP
ranges** (the addresses they forward traffic to your origin from) precisely for this.
You take that list and configure the origin firewall to **allow 80/443 only from
those ranges and deny the rest**.

With a fictional documentation range `203.0.113.0/24` standing in for the CDN's, the
shape of the origin firewall is:

```
# Only the CDN can talk to the origin on 80/443.
allow from 203.0.113.0/24 to any port 80,443 proto tcp
# Everything else: denied.
deny  from any           to any port 80,443 proto tcp
```

Now the attacker's direct connection reaches a port that doesn't answer it: it falls
into the `deny`. The only path to the origin is again **through the CDN**, which is
where the WAF lives. The perimeter is now consistent with what you thought you had.

### A second layer: origin authentication

The IP allowlist is good, but IPs can be spoofed and the CDN's ranges are shared by
many customers. So add **authenticated origin pull**: the CDN presents a client
certificate when it connects, and the origin **only accepts TLS if that certificate
is the CDN's**. That way the origin doesn't trust "it comes from a range", but "it
comes authenticated as my CDN". It's layered defense: IP filtering at the firewall,
and cryptographic verification at the TLS layer.

## Why changing the IP isn't the fix

The temptation is: "they found my IP, I'll change it and I'm done". It doesn't last.
As soon as the new origin answers on 443, the scanners find it again; the next
certificate you issue shows up in the Certificate Transparency logs; passive DNS
records any resolution that leaks. Hiding the IP is obfuscation, and obfuscation
undoes itself over time. The only thing that **holds** is the rule enforced at the
origin's edge: if only the CDN can connect, it doesn't matter how many discover the
IP. You don't hide it: you make it useless to everyone but the CDN.

DealHive learned this with `edge-eu`: putting the CDN in front wasn't enough. Until
the origin firewall was locked down to the CDN's ranges —and the origin certificate
was added— the WAF was a door with a guard next to an open window.
